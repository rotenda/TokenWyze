The $29 Billion Moment: Why 2026 Is the Year Tokenisation Stops Being Experimental

The tokenised real-world asset market has grown 300-fold in six years and just crossed 29 billion US dollars. The IMF has called it a structural reconfiguration of global finance. Here is what is driving it, what it means, and where South Africa fits.

April 2026 11 min read RWA · Global Markets · South Africa · Regulation

On 2 April 2026, the International Monetary Fund published a note entitled "Tokenized Finance." Its central argument was deliberate and precise: tokenisation is not a marginal efficiency improvement to existing financial infrastructure. It is a fundamental reconfiguration of how trust, settlement, and risk management are organised across the global financial system. When the IMF uses the word reconfiguration, it is worth paying attention.

From Proof of Concept to Core Infrastructure

The tokenised real-world asset market grew from roughly 85 million US dollars in 2020 to over 29 billion dollars by the first quarter of 2026. That is a more than 300-fold increase in six years. More importantly, the nature of that growth has fundamentally changed.

In 2023 and 2024, most tokenisation activity was experimental. Pilots, proof-of-concepts, sandbox tests. Institutions were asking whether tokenisation could work. By early 2026, that question has been answered. Institutions are now asking which products to launch first, which chains to deploy on, and which distribution channels to prioritise. The proof-of-concept era is over. Infrastructure is now the work.

"In 2025, RWA tokenisation proved it could solve the accessibility problem for asset owners. In 2026, success will be measured by whether these assets can deliver continuous market liquidity beyond the initial issuance stage."

The institutions driving this shift are not fringe players. BlackRock's tokenised money market fund, BUIDL, crossed 2 billion US dollars in assets under management. JPMorgan launched its OnChain Net Yield Fund in January 2026 with an initial seed of 100 million dollars. Franklin Templeton's tokenised money market fund became accessible through regulated platforms across multiple jurisdictions. Siemens issued a 300 million Euro corporate bond directly on-chain. These are not experiments. They are live products with real capital from real investors.

The Regulatory Shift That Changed Everything

No single factor has done more to accelerate institutional tokenisation in 2026 than regulatory clarity. Three developments in the United States during the first quarter of 2026 have materially reshaped the landscape.

The banking regulators clarified capital treatment

On 5 March 2026, the Federal Reserve, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency, and the FDIC published a joint statement making their position explicit: the capital rule is technology neutral. A tokenised security receives the same capital treatment as the non-tokenised form of the same security. This removed the regulatory uncertainty that had led many banks to treat tokenised versions of familiar instruments as novel assets requiring entirely new capital analysis. In practical terms, it means a bank can now hold a tokenised US Treasury and treat it exactly like the paper equivalent.

The SEC and CFTC named digital commodities

On 17 March 2026, the SEC and CFTC issued a joint interpretation classifying sixteen crypto assets as digital commodities rather than securities. This provides the commodity versus security classification framework that the US CLARITY Act is designed to enshrine in statute. The bill, which passed the House in July 2025 and cleared the Senate Agriculture Committee in January 2026, is targeted for Senate Banking Committee markup in late April. Its passage would make the March classification resistant to reversal by future administrations.

The GENIUS Act established stablecoin rules

The GENIUS Act, passed in 2025, established the first federal regulatory framework for stablecoins. Issuers must now back stablecoins with 100% reserves, substantiated by mandatory monthly disclosures. This matters enormously for tokenisation because stablecoins are the settlement layer for tokenised assets. Without a regulated, trusted stablecoin framework, tokenised asset markets cannot scale. The GENIUS Act provides that foundation.

The New Frontier: Liquidity, Not Just Issuance

The tokenisation industry's focus has shifted in 2026. The first wave was about proving that assets could be issued on-chain. That has been demonstrated. The second wave, which is now underway, is about secondary market liquidity. Tokenising an asset and putting it on a blockchain does not automatically make it liquid. Most tokenised RWA tokens still exhibit low trading volumes, long holding periods, and limited investor participation.

The new challenge is building the buyer side. Without a sufficiently broad and active investor base, secondary markets cannot develop the depth needed to make tokenised assets genuinely more liquid than their traditional equivalents. The NYSE and Nasdaq moves toward 24/7 tokenised trading venues are directly aimed at solving this. If you can trade a tokenised stock at any hour, from any timezone, using any regulated platform, the liquidity constraints that have historically limited tokenised markets start to dissolve.

Programmable compliance is also emerging as a key feature of the 2026 landscape. Instead of manually checking compliance at each stage of a transaction, the compliance logic is embedded directly into the smart contract. Every trade becomes auditable and compliant by design, rather than by manual intervention after the fact. This is what the market calls programmable trust, and it is one of the most significant structural advantages tokenised markets have over traditional ones.

What Is Being Tokenised Right Now

Government bonds and fixed income: the largest category

Tokenised US Treasuries represent approximately 45% of the total tokenised RWA market, crossing 12 billion dollars in 2026. They are the most straightforward assets to tokenise: smart contracts can ensure each tokenised Treasury is backed by a real one, interest payments can be automated, and settlement times collapse from two days to seconds. The depth of the US Treasury market, at nearly 28 trillion dollars, means that even at current tokenisation rates, the runway for growth is almost unlimited.

Private credit: the fastest-growing category

Private credit tokenisation is growing faster than any other segment. Protocols like Centrifuge are enabling the tokenisation of loans, invoices, and private debt, bringing an asset class that was previously accessible only to large institutional investors to a much broader pool of capital. The total value of tokenised private credit exceeded 3 billion dollars in early 2026 and is projected to grow significantly as more asset managers adopt on-chain credit infrastructure.

Tokenised commodities: gold leading the way

PAX Gold, a token where each coin represents one troy ounce of physical gold held in allocated London vaults, crossed 2.5 billion dollars in market capitalisation in March 2026. Gold is the leading tokenised commodity, but the model is being extended to energy products, agricultural commodities, and industrial metals. For commodity-rich economies, this category is particularly significant.

South Africa: Where Does This Leave Us?

South Africa is not watching from the sidelines. The country has been building its regulatory and institutional foundations for tokenised markets more systematically than most of its peers, and 2026 is the year those foundations are being tested against a global market that is now moving at serious speed.

The FSCA's licensing progress

By December 2024, the FSCA had received 420 crypto asset service provider licence applications, approving 248 and declining nine. The regulator has taken a deliberately thorough approach: on-site inspections, assessment of business models, evaluation of governance frameworks and AML capabilities. Tokenisation services are specifically included among the business models subject to this rigorous assessment. The FSCA has also published a three-year regulation plan through to 2027, with a broader overhaul of the regulatory regime expected under the Conduct of Financial Institutions Bill. The direction is clear: South Africa is building a framework designed to last.

The JSE is watching closely

At the JSE Trade Connect Conference in February 2026, digital asset tokenisation was named one of the three pivotal themes for South Africa's capital markets. Vuyo Mashiqa, head of equities and equity derivatives at the JSE, noted the rise of tokenised bonds and their implications for market access and settlement. The conference brought together the SARB Deputy Governor, the FSCA Deputy Commissioner, and senior figures from The Presidency to address structural reforms and what they mean for South Africa's attractiveness to global capital.

"We are starting to see a rise in retail investors, particularly given developments around digital assets, digital currencies and tokenisation. All of these are important enablers for clients."

The stablecoin question

The FSCA issued a comprehensive request for information to its over 240 licensed crypto asset service providers in October 2025, including specific questions on stablecoin activities and how providers comply with exchange control regulations set by the SARB. In 2026, the market is anticipating more clarity on South Africa's approach to stablecoins. This matters enormously. As the US has demonstrated with the GENIUS Act, a regulated stablecoin framework is the settlement layer that makes tokenised asset markets possible at institutional scale. South Africa needs its own equivalent.

The opportunity South Africa cannot afford to miss

South Africa holds a genuinely unusual position. It has an advanced financial market infrastructure anchored by the JSE and Strate. It has a SARB that has already run serious tokenisation experiments through Project Khokha. It has an FSCA that is building a rigorous but forward-looking licensing framework. And it has a capital-hungry economy where tokenisation's promise of democratised access and lower settlement costs could have real impact on both retail investors and SME financing.

The global tokenisation wave that is now building will not wait for regulatory frameworks to be perfect before it moves capital. Singapore, Dubai, and the EU are already attracting large concentrations of tokenised asset flows precisely because they offer the legal certainty that institutional players require. South Africa has the foundations. What it needs now is the final layer of regulatory clarity, particularly around stablecoins, security token classification under the Financial Markets Act, and the treatment of on-chain settlement finality.

The Bottom Line

The IMF called it a structural reconfiguration. Standard Chartered's CEO said the majority of transactions will eventually settle on blockchain. The NYSE is building a 24/7 tokenised trading venue. BlackRock has 2 billion dollars in a tokenised money market fund. The question of whether tokenisation would work has been answered. The question of whether it would scale is being answered right now, in real time, with real capital.

For South Africa, the urgency is real. The global tokenisation infrastructure is being built and standardised. The jurisdictions that participate actively in that process will have influence over how it works. The jurisdictions that arrive late will simply adopt what was built elsewhere. South Africa has done enough to still be in the first group. The window, however, is not unlimited.
