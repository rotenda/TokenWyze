The Institutions That Will Shape Tokenised Capital Markets in South Africa

Tokenised markets will not be built by any single entity. They will emerge from the collaboration of regulators, central banks, exchanges, banks, asset managers, and technology providers. Here is a breakdown of who plays what role.

March 2026 9 min read FSCA · JSE · SARB · Tokenisation · South Africa

The global financial system is undergoing a structural transformation as real-world assets begin migrating onto blockchain infrastructure. For South Africa, the question is no longer whether tokenisation will emerge, but which institutions will shape its development and how.

Financial Regulators: Establishing the Legal Foundation

The regulatory framework will ultimately determine how tokenised assets are classified and how market participants can interact with them. In South Africa, the primary market conduct regulator is the Financial Sector Conduct Authority.

The FSCA plays a critical role in regulating financial markets and intermediaries, protecting investors, and providing regulatory clarity for emerging financial products. In recent years, regulators globally have begun engaging more actively with digital assets. In South Africa, regulatory progress has already been made through the classification of certain crypto assets as financial products.

Key regulatory questions ahead
  • How should tokenised securities be classified under existing South African law?
  • How should digital asset platforms be regulated and licensed?
  • What investor protections should apply specifically to tokenised markets?

Regulatory clarity will be one of the most important drivers of institutional adoption. Without it, financial institutions cannot commit capital or infrastructure to markets that may be restructured by future regulation.

The Central Bank: Settlement Infrastructure and Financial Stability

The South African Reserve Bank occupies a central position in the development of tokenised financial markets. Central banks globally are exploring the use of distributed ledger technology in financial market infrastructure, and the SARB is no exception. Project Khokha 2 demonstrated that it is technically feasible to issue tokenised securities and settle them using a wholesale central bank digital currency.

Tokenised securities could fundamentally change how financial transactions settle. Instead of relying on multi-day clearing processes, blockchain-based systems could enable near-instant settlement. The SARB therefore has a direct interest in understanding the impact of tokenised assets on financial stability, how digital settlement systems interact with existing payment infrastructure, and what role digital currencies could play in supporting tokenised markets.

Stock Exchanges: The Future Marketplaces for Tokenised Securities

Stock exchanges are likely to become the primary marketplaces for tokenised financial assets. In South Africa, the Johannesburg Stock Exchange holds this position. Globally, exchanges are exploring the integration of blockchain technology into market infrastructure, with tokenised securities enabling digital issuance, blockchain-based trading platforms, and faster settlement cycles.

Tokenisation could also unlock new opportunities for exchanges: fractional ownership of assets, new categories of digital financial products, and broader global investor participation. The JSE is uniquely positioned to evolve into a hybrid financial marketplace supporting both traditional and digital securities.

Banks: Custody, Issuance, and Market Liquidity

Large banks will play a critical role in the tokenised financial ecosystem. Major South African banking groups, including Standard Bank, FirstRand, and Absa, are already exploring digital asset infrastructure and blockchain technologies. Banks are particularly important because they provide custody of financial assets, capital markets services, market liquidity, and trusted financial infrastructure.

In a tokenised environment, banks could become custodians of digital securities, issuers of tokenised financial instruments, and participants in blockchain-based settlement networks. Their participation is essential for institutional adoption. Without banking infrastructure supporting digital asset custody and settlement, tokenised markets cannot reach scale.

Asset Managers: Bringing Capital into Tokenised Markets

Asset managers control large pools of institutional capital and therefore have significant influence over the development of new financial markets. Globally, leading asset managers such as BlackRock and Franklin Templeton have already launched tokenised investment funds, signalling that tokenised products are moving from experimental to mainstream.

Tokenisation enables asset managers to explore fractional investment products, programmable financial instruments, and more efficient settlement processes. As South African asset managers begin exploring tokenised financial products, they could introduce new types of digital investment vehicles designed for both institutional and retail investors.

Blockchain Infrastructure Providers: The Technology Layer

Tokenised markets require robust technological infrastructure capable of supporting digital asset issuance, custody, and interoperability. Companies such as Chainlink are building critical infrastructure that enables financial institutions to integrate blockchain systems with existing financial architecture, providing secure smart contract execution, reliable data feeds for financial markets, and interoperability between blockchain networks.

The development of tokenised capital markets will therefore depend not only on financial institutions but also on technology providers capable of delivering secure and scalable infrastructure at the standards required for regulated markets.

Fintech Innovators: Building the Platforms of the Future

Fintech companies are likely to build many of the platforms that enable tokenised asset issuance and trading. Globally, companies such as Securitize and tZERO have developed platforms for digital securities issuance and trading, allowing companies to issue tokenised securities, manage investor onboarding, and facilitate secondary trading of digital assets.

South Africa's growing fintech ecosystem may produce similar platforms tailored to the needs of African investors and businesses. The IFWG regulatory sandbox provides a controlled environment for such platforms to develop and be tested before operating at scale.

A Collaborative Ecosystem

Tokenised financial markets will not emerge from a single institution or technology. They will be built through collaboration.

Each institution contributes a critical component of the emerging digital financial architecture. Regulators provide the legal framework. The central bank provides the settlement infrastructure. Exchanges provide the marketplace. Banks provide custody and liquidity. Asset managers bring capital. Technology providers build the rails. Fintechs build the platforms.

South Africa already has one of the most sophisticated financial systems on the African continent. With the right regulatory clarity and industry collaboration, the country could become a leading hub for tokenised financial markets in Africa. Tokenisation has the potential to unlock new forms of investment, increase market accessibility, and modernise financial market infrastructure. The institutions that recognise this opportunity early will play a defining role in shaping the future of capital markets.
