What is Tokenisation? A Simple Guide for African Markets

Tokenisation is one of the most important financial concepts of our time. It is also one of the most misunderstood. This guide explains it clearly, with examples that speak directly to the African context.

March 2026 9 min read Education · Africa · RWA · Financial Inclusion

Imagine owning a share of a Sandton office building for the price of a takeaway meal. Or a Kenyan farmer tokenising next season's harvest to access a loan today. Or a South African student in Johannesburg buying a fraction of a US government bond from her phone. This is not science fiction. This is tokenisation, and it is already happening across Africa.

Start Here: What Does Tokenisation Actually Mean?

Tokenisation is the process of converting ownership rights in a real-world asset into a digital token that lives on a blockchain. That token represents your stake in the underlying asset, whether it is a property, a government bond, a piece of gold, a business loan, or even a share in a company.

Think of it this way. When you buy a share on the Johannesburg Stock Exchange, you receive a digital record confirming that you own a piece of a company. That record sits in a centralised database managed by Strate, South Africa's central securities depository. Tokenisation does something similar, but the record lives on a blockchain instead. This changes three things fundamentally: who can participate, how quickly transactions settle, and how much the whole process costs.

A Real African Example

In April 2024, a South African private school network called Die MOS Inisiatief raised R100 million through a tokenised corporate bond issued on a blockchain platform. The minimum investment was just R5,000, roughly the price of a mid-range smartphone. Over 65% of that capital came from retail investors, including ordinary South Africans who would never have been able to participate in a traditional bond issuance. That is tokenisation working in practice.

The Problem Tokenisation Solves

Africa has one of the most compelling cases for tokenisation of any region in the world. The continent sits on enormous wealth. It holds vast mineral resources, agricultural land, infrastructure assets, and a young, growing population with rising incomes. Yet most of this wealth is locked away from ordinary people.

The reason is simple: access. Traditional financial markets have high barriers to entry. To invest in a commercial property, you typically need millions of rands. To access government bonds in most African markets, you need to go through an institutional broker with minimum investment thresholds that exclude most of the population. To send money across borders within Africa, you often pay fees of between 8 and 15 percent because of the fragmented correspondent banking system.

Africa also has a severe SME financing gap. Small and medium enterprises account for the majority of employment across the continent but face severe constraints in accessing credit. According to the International Finance Corporation and African Development Bank, that financing gap exceeds 300 billion US dollars. Traditional banks require collateral that many SMEs simply do not have.

Tokenisation is one financial innovation race where Africa could position itself to be on par with other emerging markets. Africa has the market and could go to scale much faster.

Tokenisation addresses all of these problems simultaneously. It breaks large assets into smaller, affordable pieces. It removes the intermediaries that add cost and delay. It operates 24 hours a day, seven days a week, across borders, on a phone. And it creates a transparent, auditable record of every transaction that regulators and investors can verify in real time.

How It Works: The Four Steps

Step 1: Choose the asset

Any asset with value can in principle be tokenised. The most common examples are real estate, government bonds, corporate bonds, commodities like gold and oil, private business loans, and infrastructure projects. In the African context, agricultural land, renewable energy projects, and trade receivables are also being explored as tokenisation candidates.

Step 2: Create the token

A legal and technical process converts the ownership right into a digital token on a blockchain. Each token represents a defined fraction of the asset. The token contains all the relevant information: who the issuer is, what the asset is, what rights the token holder has, and what happens at maturity or upon sale. This information is embedded in a smart contract, a piece of code that automatically executes agreed terms without requiring a human intermediary.

Step 3: Distribute and trade

Tokens are sold to investors through a platform. Because they are digital and divisible, they can be sold in very small units to very large numbers of people. Unlike traditional securities, which often require days to settle after a trade, tokenised assets can settle in seconds using blockchain technology. This is called delivery-versus-payment, and it eliminates the counterparty risk that exists in the settlement gap of traditional markets.

Step 4: Manage and mature

Smart contracts automatically handle ongoing obligations. If you hold a tokenised bond, the coupon payments are automatically distributed to your wallet on the scheduled date. If you hold a tokenised property, your share of rental income can be automatically transferred. There is no manual processing, no intermediary taking a cut, and no waiting for a cheque to clear.

Why Africa Is Uniquely Positioned

Africa did not build the legacy financial infrastructure that is now holding developed markets back. Europe and America spent decades building centralised clearinghouses, paper-based settlement systems, and correspondent banking networks that are expensive to maintain and slow to upgrade. Africa, in many cases, never built those systems at scale.

This is the same dynamic that allowed Africa to leapfrog fixed-line telecoms and go straight to mobile phones. It is the same dynamic that gave Africa M-Pesa, the world's first large-scale mobile money system, years before digital wallets existed in Europe. Kenya's M-Pesa was, in a very real sense, an early form of tokenised value: digital representations of money moving instantly, peer to peer, without a bank account. Tokenisation simply extends that logic to a far wider range of assets.

Africa's digital foundation is already in place
  • Africa has 1.1 billion registered mobile money accounts, more than half the world's total
  • Mobile money transactions across sub-Saharan Africa surpassed 700 billion US dollars in 2023
  • Africa leads the world in stablecoin adoption, with 79% of crypto-active users holding stablecoins, compared to 45% in high-income markets
  • African diaspora remittances reached 54 billion US dollars in 2023, representing pools of savings that tokenised investment products could capture
  • The tokenisation market in Africa is projected to reach 100 billion US dollars by the end of 2026, according to PwC

What Is Already Happening Across the Continent

South Africa

South Africa has the continent's most advanced financial infrastructure and is furthest along the regulatory path. The South African Reserve Bank's Project Khokha demonstrated that a tokenised government security could be issued and settled using a wholesale central bank digital currency. The Die MOS tokenised bond showed that retail investors could access corporate debt for as little as R5,000. Stablecoins have displaced Bitcoin as South Africa's most widely used digital asset, with volumes growing approximately 50% month on month since late 2024. The FSCA and National Treasury are actively working on frameworks to bring crypto assets and tokenised securities into the formal regulatory perimeter.

Kenya

Kenya's M-Pesa ecosystem gave the country a head start in digital financial infrastructure. The Nairobi Securities Exchange partnered with DeFi Technologies and other partners in 2024 to explore digital asset exchange-traded products. Kenyan fintech startups are prototyping agricultural and renewable energy asset tokens, allowing smallholders and investors to share in farm or solar yields with very small initial investments. The cultural familiarity with M-Pesa means that Kenyans already understand the concept of digital value moving instantly between people, making tokenisation a natural extension rather than a foreign concept.

Nigeria

Nigeria recorded nearly 22 billion US dollars in stablecoin transactions between mid-2023 and mid-2024, the largest volume on the continent. Stablecoins are being used for cross-border payments, remittances, and as a store of value in an environment of persistent currency pressure. The country's regulatory sandbox pilots are exploring how blockchain-based financial products can be brought into a formal framework. Given that Nigerian SMEs account for 60 to 70% of employment and nearly half of GDP, the potential for tokenised trade finance and SME credit is enormous.

Uganda

Uganda launched a central bank digital currency pilot linked to a 5.5 billion US dollar real-world asset tokenisation initiative aligned with continental trade and infrastructure objectives, one of the most ambitious tokenisation programmes announced anywhere on the continent.

The Challenges That Must Be Addressed

Tokenisation is not without risk and Africa's path will not be without obstacles. Three challenges stand above the others.

Regulation

Clear regulatory frameworks do not yet exist in most African markets for tokenised securities. Without legal clarity on what a token represents, how it is taxed, and what investor protections apply, institutional capital will stay on the sidelines. South Africa is ahead of most, but even here the frameworks are still being built. Regulators across the continent need to move from observation to active framework development without stifling innovation in the process.

Data quality and governance

A tokenised asset is only as trustworthy as the data underpinning it. If the underlying asset is misrepresented, if valuations are inaccurate, or if ownership records are disputed, the token loses its value proposition. Africa has real challenges with data infrastructure, land registries, and corporate record keeping that need to be addressed for tokenisation to function at scale.

Digital access

Tokenisation runs on smartphones and internet connections. Large parts of rural Africa still lack reliable connectivity. The financial inclusion promise of tokenisation can only be fully realised if digital infrastructure keeps pace. The good news is that mobile penetration continues to expand rapidly, and satellite internet is reaching areas that fixed-line infrastructure never will.

What This Means for You

Whether you are an investor, a business owner, a regulator, or simply a curious observer of where finance is going, tokenisation is relevant to you. It is not a distant technology reserved for Silicon Valley or Wall Street. It is already being built in Johannesburg, Nairobi, Lagos, and Kampala.

For investors, it means access to asset classes that were previously beyond reach. For businesses, it means new ways to raise capital without the gatekeepers of traditional finance. For regulators, it means both an opportunity to design a modern financial system from first principles and a responsibility to do so before informal and unregulated markets fill the gap.

Africa has the mobile infrastructure. It has the demographic scale. It has the assets. It has proven, with M-Pesa and mobile money, that it can build world-leading financial innovation when the conditions are right. Tokenisation is the next chapter of that story. The question is not whether it will happen. The question is whether Africa will lead it or follow it.
