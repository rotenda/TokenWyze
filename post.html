Project Khokha 2: How the SARB Tokenised a Security and What It Revealed About the Future of Financial Markets

A thorough examination of the South African Reserve Bank's landmark distributed ledger experiment, what was built, what was proved, and what legal, operational and policy questions remain unanswered.

February 2026 12 min read SARB · wCBDC · DLT · Capital Markets

In April 2022, the South African Reserve Bank and the Intergovernmental Fintech Working Group published the full report on Project Khokha 2, one of the most technically sophisticated central bank tokenisation experiments conducted anywhere in the world. This is a close reading of what they actually built, what worked, what did not, and why it matters.

Where It Began: Project Khokha 1

To understand Project Khokha 2 (PK2), you need to start with its predecessor. Project Khokha 1, launched in 2018, explored whether distributed ledger technology (DLT) could replicate core functions of South Africa's real-time gross settlement system, the South African Multiple Option Settlement (SAMOS) system. The answer was yes. PK1 proved that elements of SAMOS could be duplicated at scale and speed on DLT while maintaining privacy, confidentiality, and providing the SARB sufficient oversight and operational management.

That success created the foundation for a harder question: what happens when you move beyond interbank payments and attempt to tokenise an actual security, end to end, from issuance through to maturity settlement?

What PK2 Set Out to Do

Formally launched in February 2021 as an IFWG Innovation Accelerator project, PK2 focused on the tokenisation of a SARB-issued debenture, a short-term debt security used to drain excess liquidity from the domestic money markets. Debentures were deliberately chosen because they are directly under the SARB's control, making them practical to tokenise in a proof-of-concept, with the intention that insights could later be applied to other securities in different environments.

"The aim of PK2 is to highlight the policy and regulatory implications of tokenisation in financial markets based on DLT-driven innovation."

The project team was given an explicit mandate: do not simply replicate existing processes on new technology. Design for DLT. Re-imagine the market from first principles. This distinction matters, it is what separates PK2 from tokenisation experiments that digitise a workflow without questioning the underlying architecture.

PK2 was a collaborative initiative involving the SARB's Fintech Unit, Accenture and Block Markets Africa as technical service providers, and Deloitte as support partner. Active participants included Absa, FirstRand, Nedbank, Standard Bank and JSE Limited. Observers included the Financial Sector Conduct Authority, National Treasury, Strate, the Financial Intelligence Centre, and multiple divisions within the SARB itself.

The Current Debentures Market: What Was Being Replaced

To appreciate what PK2 built, it helps to understand what currently exists. In South Africa, SARB debentures are auctioned by the Financial Markets Department (FMD) every Wednesday when there is a need to drain liquidity. Bids from eligible commercial banks and asset managers are placed, FMD allocates debentures to the best bidders, and Strate, as the central securities depository (CSD), facilitates settlement. Money settlement moves through SAMOS, and the JSE assigns an ISIN to each debenture as the National Numbering Agency.

This infrastructure works, but it involves multiple separate institutions each maintaining their own ledgers, requiring reconciliation across systems, and operating with a T+0 settlement cycle for debentures. The question PK2 asked was: what if all of this could happen on a single shared DLT platform?

Two Platforms, One Debenture Token Market

The proof-of-concept was built on two distinct DLT platforms designed to interoperate, and that interoperability challenge turned out to be one of the most revealing aspects of the project.

The wCBDC Zone

The first platform was the wholesale central bank digital currency (wCBDC) Zone, built on R3 Corda. This platform was owned and operated by the SARB and served as the digital equivalent of the central bank's settlement system. The wCBDC token minted here was designed to carry a direct and fungible claim on the SARB, equivalent in value and quality to money held in a settlement account at the central bank. When a wCBDC token transferred between participants, the associated claim on the central bank transferred with it.

The Khokha Hub

The second platform, the Khokha Hub, was built on a custom implementation of the Cosmos blockchain called BitFair. This is where the debenture tokens were issued and traded. Its modular architecture enabled it to serve simultaneously as a trading platform, a CSD, a securities settlement system, and elements of a payment system, collapsing functions that currently require separate institutions onto a single decentralised platform.

The Khokha Hub was envisioned as a consortium-governed token trading platform (TTP) operated jointly by market participants through a Khokha Council. Any participant could propose new business logic as modules, comparable to decentralised applications in DeFi, which the Council would vote on whether to implement.

Three Prototypes Built in PK2
  • A wCBDC, a liability of the SARB, issued on the wCBDC Zone as an alternate form of central bank money restricted to settlement participants
  • A wholesale digital settlement token (wToken) called the Khokha Token, a commercial bank-issued stablecoin backed by funds earmarked in SAMOS settlement accounts, and redeemable for wCBDC
  • A DLT-based SARB debenture token, issued as an NFT on the Khokha Hub, designed specifically for DLT rather than as a digital copy of the existing instrument

How the Debenture Token Life Cycle Worked

The debenture token life cycle in the PoC followed the real-world process but executed key functions on-chain. FMD would announce an auction on Wednesday, receive bids from eligible participants, and issue debenture tokens as NFTs directly onto the Khokha Hub. Each NFT contained the full details of the instrument: debt type, issuer, issuing and maturity dates, interest rate, and beneficiary. An ISIN was obtained from a simulated National Numbering Agency for registration alongside this data.

Settlement in the primary market used a delivery-versus-payment (DvP) atomic swap. Three actions occurred simultaneously in a single transaction: the wCBDC moved from buyer to seller, ownership of the debenture NFT transferred to the buyer, and the NFT's data was validated against its ISIN record. If any one of the three steps failed, all three were rolled back , ensuring true DvP without the risk of one leg settling without the other.

At maturity, FMD made payment of the principal and interest owed in wCBDC, which a smart contract distributed to the relevant beneficiaries, directly equivalent to the MaFISI (Maturity Financial Instrument Settlement Instruction) process in the current market.

Fractionalised Debt at Maturity Tokens

To create liquidity in the secondary market, where high minimum values of ZAR 1 million per debenture and different maturity dates made direct trading difficult, the team created Fractionalised Debt at Maturity (FDM) Tokens. These synthetic instruments combined the principal value and interest of a debenture before maturity, making it fungible and tradeable in smaller amounts using a decentralised exchange order book on the Khokha Hub.

An unintended consequence emerged here: the FDM Tokens, representing a claim against the central bank, could themselves be used as a form of money without converting them to wCBDC or Khokha Tokens. A security had effectively become a piece of what DeFi calls "Money Lego" , a building block for further financial innovation. The report notes this with interest but also flags that it runs contrary to the purpose of debentures, which is to drain liquidity from the market, not create new forms of it.

The Software Bridge: The Most Complex Element

Enabling the wCBDC, issued on the wCBDC Zone, to be used on the Khokha Hub required a software bridge operated centrally by the SARB. When a bank wanted to buy debenture tokens with wCBDC, the wCBDC was moved to a cession wallet in the wCBDC Zone where it was frozen. An equivalent value of wCBDC was created on the Khokha Hub and credited to the bank's wallet. A strict 1:1 mapping was maintained at all times. When the bank imported wCBDC back to the wCBDC Zone, the Khokha Hub tokens were destroyed and the frozen wCBDC was released.

"Care should therefore be taken in porting wCBDC to networks not controlled by the issuing central bank."

This porting mechanism worked technically, but it exposed a significant legal tension. Under South African legislation, settlement in a designated settlement system is final and irrevocable. When wCBDC was moved to the Khokha Hub, it created a split between when operational settlement occurred and when legal settlement occurred. This is identified in the report as one of the most important unresolved questions from the project, with the recommendation that wCBDC should ideally be used on its own platform for the full benefit of both technical and legal settlement finality.

What the Report Says About Market Structure

The most consequential finding of PK2 was structural rather than technical. The Khokha Hub, in its final design, performed the functions of a trading platform, a CSD, a securities settlement system, and elements of a payment system. Multiple separate institutions currently perform these functions in South Africa. DLT collapsed them onto a single shared ledger.

The report makes an important distinction between role players and roles. The roles , issuing, trading, settling, custodying, did not disappear. But the number of entities required to perform them could be significantly reduced. Reconciliation across multiple ledgers, a major source of cost and operational risk in current markets, would be eliminated by a single shared source of truth with different levels of access depending on participant roles.

However, the report is careful to note that existing structures evolved over time to ensure the safety, efficiency, and resilience of financial markets. Some existing frictions, such as those inherent in post-trade funding, exist for business reasons. Before discarding any structure, its function and purpose must be fully understood.

The Legal and Regulatory Gaps PK2 Exposed

The report devotes significant attention to legal and regulatory questions that PK2 raised but could not answer. These are the actual barriers that would need to be resolved before any DLT-based market infrastructure could go into production in South Africa.

The wCBDC legal framework

Issuing a token-based wCBDC would require the SARB to consider its impact on its mandate and legal framework. Is it legally permissible to use a distributed ledger as a designated settlement system? What is the legal status of a wCBDC wallet as an account with the central bank? What is the viability of designating the wCBDC's DLT as a settlement system? These questions remain open.

Security tokens and the Financial Markets Act

Security tokens are not defined in the Financial Markets Act 19 of 2012. The regulatory treatment of a tokenised SARB debenture, or any tokenised security, is therefore uncertain. The report calls for the FMA to be updated to enable an agile and adaptive framework for emerging asset classes, ensuring legislation is technology neutral and principle based so that regulators can respond to changing market conditions without requiring constant legislative amendment.

The wToken and stablecoin regulation

The Khokha Token, as a stablecoin used for wholesale settlement, would need to align with broader regulatory approaches to stablecoins. The report flags several Principles for Financial Market Infrastructures (PFMIs) that present specific compliance challenges, including PFMI 1 (enforceable legal basis), PFMI 2 (clear and transparent governance), PFMI 8 (settlement finality), and PFMI 9 (settlement in central bank money where practical and available). At present, the FMA does not clearly define how a wToken would be treated in law.

Four Directions for Further Exploration

The PK2 report identified four broad paths forward, drawn directly from its conclusions:

  • A DLT innovation acceleration platform with industry, allowing financial institutions to explore DLT applications under regulatory oversight for an extended period
  • Collaboration with other jurisdictions, building on the SARB's participation in Project Dunbar to explore multi-wCBDC arrangements, potentially within the SADC region
  • A new phase of Project Khokha to build on PK2, potentially performing live transactions in a sandbox environment using a different use case
  • Desktop research to explore the policy and regulatory implications of DLT-driven financial market innovation in more depth

Why This Matters

PK2 is not a theoretical exercise. It involved the SARB's own Financial Markets Department issuing real debenture tokens, major commercial banks as active participants, and the JSE, Strate, and multiple regulatory bodies as observers. The findings carry institutional weight.

The report's central insight is that the true benefit and challenge of DLT may not stem from the technology itself, but from the business process innovation it enables. Moving to DLT-based financial markets does not simply speed up existing processes, it forces a rethinking of which processes are necessary, who should perform them, and what the correct legal and governance architecture looks like in a tokenised world.

South Africa, the report notes, faces an opportune moment. Legislative reforms in both the payments and financial markets space are already underway. The window to embed technology-neutral, principle-based frameworks capable of accommodating tokenised markets is open. PK2 has continued to provide a collaborative space for experimentation with DLT within the industry, contributing to a growing body of knowledge. What follows from here will depend on the willingness of policymakers, regulators, and industry to move from experimentation to action.
